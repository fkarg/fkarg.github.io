1. Use Checklists
If you do something regularly, or depend upon doing something with a
sufficiently high enough quality, then you should create a checklist for it.
Not because you will need it, but so that you can use it, and depend upon it.
The biggest benefit is probably reduced stress - you won't forget some
important step now. That's also a great benefit, even if you could redo that
step later. Pilots are using checklists for everything, resulting in them
rarely forgetting something or making a mistake. How often do you forget
something? How often do you make a mistake, that would have been caught with a
list? Do you really think you can do better than a pilot under stress?
bonus: you can 

2. Create Systems and Processes
Checklists are good for tasks that are simple and easy. What happens after this
checklist? when do I apply this one, when the other one? Create systems and
processes to offload situational computation in the past. You don't have to be
too precise most of the time, but the results improve with clarity of application.

The most important parts: measure efficiency and have a feedback loop to adapt
if useful (instead of: if necessary).

3. Forgetting the Reason
It's easy to get lost in details when the only thing you do each day is make
progress on some small indicator, and that's all that matters for you. Why does
it matter for you? what does this indicator say about your work and how does it
affect others? Every now and then, take your time to figure out how things are
connected, how the big picture looks like, and how your part affects the
others. It's likely you'll see a few things you could change. Tell others too.
