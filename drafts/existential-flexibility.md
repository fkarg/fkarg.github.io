## Intelligence as a measure for Adaptability
I heard about multiple definitions of intelligence, but there is one that got
stuck:

> Intelligence is the degree of flexibility in both learning and behaviour.

Honestly, thinking about it, it makes sense. Especially when trying to evaluate
non-human forms of intelligence. A dog won't be able to do well on an IQ test,
but we would still argue that most dogs are intelligent. Same with apes or
dolphins - apparently dolphins are the most intelligent species after humans
(search for source/look that up!), but then how would you actually measure it?

The degree of flexibility in both learning and behaviour is a good
indicator for capability in artificial intelligence as well. Support Vector
Machines (SVM) (link?) work quite well for some problems, so they are clearly
adaptable enough to learn those. But Artificial Neural Networks seem to be able
to adapt to a much wider range of problems, and they clearly outperform SVMs in
most tasks, if not all. So based on their capability, it makes sense to
classify them as 'slightly more intelligent', though of course both have their
own significant shortcomings.


## Flexibility
Flexibility and adaptability are roughly the capability to adapt to
environmental circumstances. For example, when the train you were supposed to
take gets cancelled, you adapt to the situation by searching for the next
connection taking you to your destination. Similarly, when you feed your dog,
the dog easily adapts to the situation by eating. In fact, we usually adapt to
every situation in one way or another.

However, we are not infinitely flexible. If someone requests that you listen to
their talk while someone else needs you in a meeting at the same time, there is
no way we can 'adapt' accordingly to fulfill both requests. You would need a
[clone of yourself][replication] to fulfill both conditions.


## Existential Flexibility
Existential Flexibility takes adaptability to the extreme. It describes the
capability to (among other things) be flexible about _existential_ reasons. For
people, it would be the capacity to change a career, or an identity.  For
organisations it is the capacity and ability to completely change _what they
do_. Not from manufacturing fighter jets to passenger airplanes, but from
manufacturing fighter jets to a nonprofit organisation trying to eradicate
malaria in africa. It is important that the capability and capacity would
exist, not that such _existential_ changes happen frequently.


## Why
You might ask yourself: "Sure, existential flexibility probably exists and all,
but why would it be important? And how is it related to intelligence?" We'll
get to that.

In 1997, Apple was on the verge of bankruptcy when Steve Jobs became CEO again.
In March 1998 he radically reduced Apples lineup. Among other things, they
offered twelve different printers, all similar in capability and price. He cut
that down to one. (read again about the changes he made in detail)

When asked why he did that, since that would probably destroy apple, his answer was:
> Better we blow it up ourselves, than someone else - Steve Jobs

This, in combination with a few other similarly radical changes allowed apple
to sustain until they created the iPod, from which it only went upward. It was
not guaranteed that Apple would survive if they made these radical changes, but
it was guaranteed that they would not be able to hold on for much longer. So
yes, in the end, these changes _saved_ apple, and allowed it to become what it
is today.

But there are other situations we can apply this to as well: when making
mistakes. Our world is sufficiently complex that it is inevitable that we make
mistakes every now and then.

We are all capable of _existential_ changes. Your childhood and early adulthood
are probably full of them. Traumas and Massive mistakes included, we all did
something stupid once and learned a big lesson on what _not_ to do, which
fundamentally changed our approach to something. That might not have been an
existential change, but a fairly fundamental one.

Since we made them before, and we will continue to make them, how about we try
to learn as much as possible as fast as possible while we are at it?
