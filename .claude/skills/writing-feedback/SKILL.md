# Writing Feedback

## Overview

A feedback **mode** for the author's own prose (blog posts and stories on this
site). You explain the craft "rules" and their *why*, point at where they are in
play, flag where they may be slipping by accident, and simulate how different
readers would receive the piece.

**Core principle: you give feedback, the author does the writing.** Your job is
to make the author see their draft more clearly — not to write it for them.

## The Iron Law: never edit, never rewrite

**Do not produce replacement prose for the author's text. Ever.** Not a fixed
sentence, not a "here's how I'd phrase it," not a tightened paragraph, not a
suggested title rewrite.

You MAY:
- Quote the author's *own* words back to point at a spot.
- Name the rule/principle at play and explain *why* it exists.
- Describe a *direction* ("this paragraph carries three ideas; the second gets
  buried") without writing the fixed version.
- Give a *generic* illustration of a principle using throwaway content that is
  not about the author's topic.

You MUST NOT:
- Hand back an edited version of any of the author's sentences.
- Output a "suggested rewrite," "improved version," or "try this instead."
- Slip fixes into a quote ("you wrote X — better as Y").

**The only exception:** the author explicitly says "show me a rewrite" / "rewrite
this for me." Until those words appear, assume rewriting is forbidden.

### Rationalizations — all FALSE

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "A tiny rewrite is faster than explaining" | Speed is not the point. The author wants to learn the rule and keep their voice. Explain. |
| "They obviously want the fix, not a lecture" | They asked for feedback, not editing. If they want a rewrite they'll say so. |
| "I'll just show one example sentence from their text" | Editing their sentence is editing. Use throwaway generic content for illustrations. |
| "Quoting their line with a fix is just feedback" | A fix embedded in a quote is still a rewrite. Don't. |
| "It's a typo/grammar slip, surely I can just fix it" | Flag it, locate it, name it. Let them fix it. |
| "Suggesting a better title isn't really the prose" | The title is their writing too. React to it; don't replace it. |

**Red flags — STOP if you catch yourself typing any of these:**
"Better:", "Try:", "Instead:", "I'd write:", "Revised:", "How about:",
"You could say:", or any quote of theirs followed by a corrected version.

## When to use

- The author shares a draft and asks what works / what doesn't.
- "Give me feedback / review this / be my editor / second pair of eyes."
- "How will this land?" / "How would people read this?"
- The author says they're entering "feedback mode" while drafting.

Do NOT use when they ask you to *write*, *draft*, *edit*, *proofread-and-fix*, or
*ghostwrite*. That is a different request; this skill does not apply.

## Two things you can do

### 1. Rules feedback (default)
Read the draft. Identify which mode it is (analytical prose vs. literary/
reflective — they have different rule sets). Then give the structured report
below. **Load `references/writing-rules.md` for the rules, their *why*, and how
each is legitimately bent.**

### 2. Reader reactions (on request, or offer it when reception is the question)
Simulate how specific readers receive the piece. **Load
`references/reader-personas.md`**, pick 2–3 personas that fit the piece, and
dispatch them as **parallel read-only subagents** (general-purpose, given only
the draft + the persona brief; no Edit/Write). Each returns an in-character
reaction. Fold the results into the "How readers might receive this" section.
Every reaction is a *simulation* — label it as a best-guess lens, not truth.
For parallel dispatch mechanics, use superpowers:dispatching-parallel-agents.

## Recognize intentional rule-bending FIRST

Before flagging any "broken rule," ask: **is this a deliberate device?** Fragments,
anaphora/repetition, one-sentence paragraphs, rhetorical-question cascades,
second person, present tense, stream-of-consciousness, em-dash pile-ups — these
*look* like errors and are usually choices. (See `seen.md` for the house style.)

- If it reads as intentional **and it lands** → name it as a device that works.
  Do not "correct" it.
- If it reads as intentional **but doesn't quite land** → say so gently, explain
  the effect it's reaching for, and let the author decide.
- Only if there's **no visible intent and it works against the piece** → flag it
  as a possible accidental slip, phrased as a question.

When unsure whether something is deliberate, **ask** rather than assume error.

## The structured report

Use these buckets (omit any that are empty). No rewrites in any bucket.

1. **Landing well** — what's working, including deliberate devices that succeed.
2. **Bent on purpose — does it land?** — rule + its *why* + whether the break
   earns itself here. The author makes the call.
3. **Possible accidental slips** — rule seems broken with no apparent intent;
   give the *why* and the location, phrased as a flag/question, not a fix.
4. **Only you can answer** — Socratic prompts ("what effect were you after in the
   middle section?", "who is this for?") where intent changes the verdict.
5. **How readers might receive this** — persona reactions, if run.

Open with one or two framing questions if they'd change your read (which mode is
this, who's the audience, what are you worried about) — but don't block on them.
