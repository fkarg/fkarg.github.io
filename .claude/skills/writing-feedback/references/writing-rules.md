# Writing rules — and how they're legitimately bent

Each rule is: **the rule → the *why* → how it's legitimately played with.** Give
the author the *why* so they can decide. Never present a rule as dogma, and never
fix the text — name the rule and point at where it's in play.

A rule is only worth raising if you can say what it *buys* the reader. If you
can't articulate the why, don't raise it.

---

## Part 1 — Analytical prose (essays, book reviews, trip reports, arguments)

### Omit needless words
- **Why:** "Vigorous writing is concise" (Strunk). Every word the reader processes
  should earn its place; clutter buries the signal.
- **Bent:** Deliberate redundancy for rhythm or emphasis; a long, accumulating
  sentence that *enacts* sprawl or overwhelm. Repetition that builds a drumbeat is
  not clutter.

### Prefer concrete and specific over abstract
- **Why:** "The surest way to arouse and hold attention is by being specific,
  definite and concrete" (Strunk). Particulars call up pictures; abstractions
  evaporate. Orwell: reach meaning through pictures and sensations first.
- **Bent:** Some philosophical/conceptual pieces genuinely operate at altitude;
  abstraction is the subject. Even then, one concrete anchor usually pays off.

### Favor the active voice
- **Why:** Active tends to be shorter and stronger; the agent is visible — "brevity
  is a by-product of vigor" (Strunk).
- **Bent:** Passive is *correct* when the receiver of the action matters more than
  the actor, when the actor is unknown/irrelevant, or to steer the sentence's
  emphasis. The rule is "limit passive to where it's most effective," not "never."

### Reader-expectation (Gopen & Swan)
- **Why:** Readers attach predictable meaning to sentence *positions*. Subject and
  verb close together and early; put familiar/old information at the start of a
  sentence and the new, emphatic information in the **stress position** at the end.
  Misplaced emphasis makes correct prose still feel hard.
- **Bent:** Front-loading the punch (loose → periodic inversion) for shock or pace.
  A choice, when the jolt is the point.

### One idea per paragraph; topic does real work
- **Why:** Paragraphs are the reader's unit of digestion. A paragraph carrying
  three ideas hides two of them.
- **Bent:** A single-sentence paragraph as a deliberate beat/emphasis (common in
  this site's style). That's rhythm, not a violation.

### Earn the opening
- **Why:** The first lines decide whether the reader continues. They should create
  a question, tension, or concrete image — not warm up.
- **Bent:** A slow, atmospheric open is legitimate in reflective pieces where mood
  *is* the hook.

### Signpost the structure
- **Why:** In longer analytical pieces, headings and transitions let the reader
  hold the shape. Orientation reduces load.
- **Bent:** Withholding structure to create discovery/disorientation — only if that
  experience is the intent.

### Vary sentence length; avoid a string of loose sentences
- **Why:** Monotone rhythm dulls attention; a run of "and… and… and…" loose
  sentences feels slack (Strunk).
- **Bent:** A deliberate run of short declaratives for staccato tension, or
  polysyndeton ("and… and… and") to evoke accumulation/breathlessness.

### Orwell's six rules (the analytical backbone)
1. Never use a metaphor/simile you're used to seeing in print (dead metaphors hide
   thought).
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If you can cut a word, cut it.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use jargon/foreign/technical words if an everyday equivalent exists.
6. **"Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous."**
- **Why rule 6 matters:** Orwell built the escape clause into his own list. The
  rules serve clarity and honesty; when obeying a rule produces something stilted
  or false, the rule loses. Apply this lens to *every* rule here.

## Part 2 — Story / literary / reflective writing

### Show, don't tell
- **Why:** Revealing through action, sensation and detail lets the reader *conclude*
  the emotion rather than being told it — that's what makes it felt and trusted.
- **Bent (telling is right when):** bridging time, summarizing the unimportant,
  flash/compressed forms, when showing would obscure essential info, to avoid
  purple over-writing, or as a stylistic voice (fable/fairytale cadence). The real
  question is never "show or tell?" but "what does each do for the narration here?"

### Point of view consistency
- **Why:** A stable POV is a contract; unmotivated head-hopping disorients and
  breaks immersion.
- **Bent:** Multiple POVs for genuine structural need; a brief excursion into an
  unorthodox POV — kept short and deliberate. Make the shift a *choice* the reader
  can feel is controlled.

### Scene vs. summary
- **Why:** Scene (moment-by-moment) creates immediacy and is where emotional weight
  lives; summary covers ground efficiently. Putting the important thing in summary
  wastes it; dramatizing the trivial drags.
- **Bent:** Summary *as* voice (a wry narrator compressing events) when the telling
  itself is the pleasure.

### Voice and "invisibility" (Elmore Leonard)
- **Why:** Leonard's rules aim to keep the writer invisible so the characters carry
  it; his meta-rule: "if it sounds like writing, I rewrite it."
- **Bent:** Leonard's own escape hatch — "if you have a facility for language and
  the sound of your voice pleases you, invisibility is not what you are after." A
  strong, present narrative voice is a legitimate aim, not a failure.

### Rhythm, repetition, and fragments as devices
- **Why:** Anaphora (repeated openings), fragments, and one-line paragraphs create
  cadence, emphasis, and breath. They are tools, not errors — when controlled.
- **Bent / failure line:** It works when the pattern is *felt as designed*; it fails
  when it reads as the writer losing control or padding. Judge by whether the
  repetition still earns its place by its last appearance.

### Playing with chronology
- **Why:** Linear time is the default the reader assumes.
- **Bent:** Starting at the end, flashbacks, framing — all standard. Even full
  reverse order works when emotional intensity supersedes chronological order. The
  test is whether the reader stays oriented enough to feel the effect.

### Breaking the fourth wall
- **Why:** Most prose preserves the fiction that it isn't constructed; calling
  attention to it is jarring by default.
- **Bent:** A long tradition (Don Quixote, Calvino). Works only if used
  *consistently* as a mode, not tried once and dropped.

### The meta-rule for all of Part 2
**Make it a choice, not a mistake.** A broken rule should serve the piece and be
defensible. Know the rule and its why *so you know when you're breaking it.* Never
break a rule for shock value alone.

## Part 3 — Reading rule-bending (how to tell device from error)

Things that look like mistakes but are usually deliberate. Before flagging, run
each against its "works when / fails when" test.

| Device | Looks like | Works when | Fails when |
|--------|-----------|------------|------------|
| Sentence fragment | Grammar error | Lands a beat, emphasis, or fits voice | Reader stumbles, can't parse, no payoff |
| Anaphora / repetition | Padding / monotony | Builds rhythm, escalates | Feels like the writer is stalling |
| One-sentence paragraph | Underdeveloped | Isolates a turn for impact | Used so often it loses force |
| Em-dash pile-up | Messy punctuation | Mimics live thought / asides | Reader loses the main clause |
| Polysyndeton / asyndeton | Run-on / comma splice | Evokes accumulation or speed | Reads as carelessness |
| Second person ("you") | POV slip | Implicates the reader directly | Unclear who "you" is |
| Present tense in past narrative | Tense error | Immediacy, a vivid pulled-forward moment | Inconsistent without reason |
| Rhetorical-question cascade | Evasive / no answers | Enacts searching, doubt, longing | Substitutes for having a point |
| Starting with "And"/"But" | Grammar taboo | Conversational momentum | — (this taboo is mostly a myth) |

**When the works/fails call is genuinely ambiguous, ask the author what they were
after.** Intent changes the verdict, and you can't always read it from the page.

## Sources
- Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style* — concision, concrete language, active
  voice, loose sentences.
- George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" — the six rules + escape clause.
- William Zinsser, *On Writing Well* — strip every sentence to its cleanest
  components.
- George Gopen & Judith Swan, "The Science of Scientific Writing" — reader-
  expectation, stress position, old-before-new.
- Elmore Leonard, "10 Rules of Good Writing" — invisibility, "if it sounds like
  writing, I rewrite it," the facility-with-language escape hatch.
- General fiction-craft consensus on show-don't-tell, POV, chronology, and the
  fourth wall — "know the rules and their why, then break them for a reason."
