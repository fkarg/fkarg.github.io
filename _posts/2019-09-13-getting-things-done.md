## Part 1: The Art of Getting Things Done

### Chapter one: Busy Lifes

First, he gives an introduction in how and why most of probably aren't as productive as we could be. He promises not to provide more than is known by common sense already, but rather a structured way on how to apply it. His examples of stressed everyday life are probably more accurate than some of us are aware of. It certainly applied to me now and then.


There was this one quote (there are one or more quotes on each page):

> The winds and waves are always on the side of the ablest navigators. - Edward Gibbon

Honestly, that got me thinking. I mean the funniest part of it is that is 'true', or rather, that it might seem that way. The core idea here is that for the 'ablest navigators', it doesn't matter how the wind and waves are, as they can utilize them well. For the novice, it seems as they are 'on their side', however, but they simply know much better how to deal with it. That got me wondering, as it applies to all problem types. The unexperienced is unlikely to notice the intricacies that _are_ there, and thus it seems that everything is going for the expert, the 'able', and much more so for the 'ablest'.

They are capable of modulating for the smallest of details, for which a considerable amount of experience to draw from is required. So if something appears to be going smooth for someone else, think yourself in his situation. Are you able to deal with it in the same manner? Would your actions have resulted in the same as his did? If not, are yours better or worse? If better, maybe think about it again, as it might just appear that way. If worse, you can likely learn something from this person, by asking why he modulated like that, this person just might be one of the ablest navigators. Next time, I just might take that chance to learn something.

### Chapter one: New Practice for a New Reality (Take II)

There is only one big point stressed in chapter one, that even though most people write to-do lists, they write them without being able to release the tension from the not-yet-having-thought-about-it.

He claims it is always due to at least one of these three things: 
- It is not captured by an external trusted collection tool. (Which is why you still think about it)
- It is not clear what still needs to be done to finish this 'thing'.
- There are no organized reminders somwhere.

Turns out, it is at least somewhat true for my case. Apparently I did better than others he is writing about (they only write down the general areas the tension comes from, if at all), but I can see that that might help me.

For the rest of the chapter, he repeatedly focuses on these three points, to describe better what he means by that.
