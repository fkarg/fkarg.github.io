## Part 1: The Art of Getting Things Done

### Chapter one: Busy Lifes

First, he gives an introduction in how and why most of probably aren't as productive as we could be. He promises not to provide more than is known by common sense already, but rather a structured way on how to apply it. His examples of stressed everyday life are probably more accurate than some of us are aware of. It certainly applied to me now and then.


There was this one quote (there are one or more quotes on each page):

> The winds and waves are always on the side of the ablest navigators.

Honestly, that got me thinking. I mean the funniest part of it is that is 'true', or rather, that it might seem that way. The core idea here is that for the 'ablest navigators', it doesn't matter how the wind and waves are, as they can utilize them well. For the novice, it seems as they are 'on their side', however, but they simply know much better how to deal with it. That got me wondering, as it applies to all problem types. The unexperienced is unlikely to notice the intricacies that _are_ there, and thus it seems that everything is going for the expert, the 'able', and much more so for the 'ablest'.

They are capable of modulating for the smallest of details, for which a considerable amount of experience to draw from is required. So if something appears to be going smooth for someone else, think yourself in his situation. Are you able to deal with it in the same manner? Would your actions have resulted in the same as his did? If not, are yours better or worse? If better, maybe think about it again, as it might just appear that way. If worse, you can likely learn something from this person, by asking why he modulated like that, this person just might be one of the ablest navigators. Here is your chance to learn something from them.
