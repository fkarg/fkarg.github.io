Review of Peter Thiels' 'rare thing: a concise [and] thought-provoking book on entrepreneurship'.

So I got this book some time ago, and I finally got around to reading it. Here
are my thoughts about it, roghly ordered semantically.


# Core Key Takeaways


## 1 Challange of the Future
- Starts out with: 'the next steve jobs would not build apple' and 'the next bill gates will not build microsoft'.
- 'What important truth do very few people agree with you on'.
- vertical vs horizontal progress
- his answer: 'most people think that the future of the world will be defined by globalisation, but the truth is that technology matters more.'
- own answer: you can create your own circumstances, no free will / less individuality than most are aware of / much more influenced from other people / you also have responsibility for other people
- small groups necessary, because they can move faster and they need to question everything that was before them

## 2 Party Like it's 1999
- first step to clear thinking: question what you know about the past
- dotcom-bubble history and (especially) mania
- dotcom financing weirdness (5Million before having a contract / serious consideration of fermi estimates from newspapers)
- four big lessens learned:
    - Small incremental advances
    - lean and flexible iteration
    - improve on recognizable products / create something for which there are customers
    - Focus on the product, not on sales

- however, probably the actual ones are:
>   - better to risk boldness than triviality
>   - bad plan better than no plan
>   - competitive markets destroy profits
>   - sales matters just as much as product

> how much of what you know about business is shaped by mistaken reactions to past mistakes?

## 3 All Happy Companies are Different
- what valuable company is nobody building?
- perfect competition vs monopolies -> those claiming to have the market for themselves usually don't have it.
> monopolists can afford to think about other things than just making money
- monopoly profits are a massive incentive to innovate
- competitive equilibrium similar to heat death of the universe
> Monopoly is the condition of every successful business

## 4 The Ideology of Competition
- competition is ideology permeated in society
- <entities> lose sight of what matters when they compete too much / imitative competition on other, non-owned markets
> the focus was not on productivity, the focus was on beating the enemy

Quote from my dad:
> If you have a clearly defined enemy, the structure of your day is clear

merging/unification is possible, but might require bigger forces

## 5 Last Mover Advantage

- valuations are mainly based on potential future earnings, not current ones
> will this business still be around a decade from now?
- potential durability lies in one or more of:
    - propreatary technology being at least 10x better than competition
    - network effects: social pressure to use it/it becomes better by more people using it
    - economies of scale / potential for easy massive scaling (software is easier than training people)
    - branding / create a strong brand with values etc

- how to build a monopoly
    - start with a very small market and dominate it
    - scale to adjacent markets fast
    - do try not to disrupt sleeping giants

> you must study the endgame before everything else

## 6 You Are Not a Lottery Ticket

- chance is not take part in success, as everyone can create chances
- priorities good planning and good design above all else

## 7 Follow the Money

- compound interest / power law
- VC: do invest only if the company has the potential to become more valuable than the entire fund

## 8 Secrets

> Contrarian thinking does not make any sense unless the world still has secrets left to give up
- difficult things are still be achievable / easy, hard and impossible problems
- you need to look for secrets, if you want to find them
- two types of secrets (valuable for businesses):
    - what secrets is nature not telling you?
    - what secrets are people not telling you?
- whom to tell secrets? whover you need to, and no more

## 9 Foundations

> a startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed

- founders should have a history of working together bevor the company, and have complimentary skillsets
- how to divide ownership, possession and control
- boards with no more than three to five people
- a company does better the less it pays to the ceo
- openness to invention is one of the most critical 'traits'

## 10 The Mechanics of Mafia

- what does the ideal workplace look like? things with substance (actual culture, the not-tabletennis-parts) are more important
- you need more than professional contact for a good business
- you hire people for a conspiracy 
- why should the 20th employee join your company? -> good answers are specific to your company
- have answers to 'mission and team'
> everyone in your company should be different in the same way

> every individual should be sharply distinguished by their work -> evaluate them for one thing only

> [...] you don't need to worry if your company does not make sense to conventional professionals. Better to be called a cult - or even a mafia.

## 11 If You Build It, Will They Come?

- salespeople and other 'middlemen' supposedly get in the way
- it is hard to make sales look easy
- people do not like 'being sold', so the are called 'account executives, business development, investment bankers, or polictians'
- build a product that will sell itself ... as soon as people hear about it
- good enterprise sales strategies start small ... as they must. To get references fast, then scale
- do not try to compete in marketing with bigger companies (they are bound to be much better)
- get _one_ distribution channel right

> everybody sells. Look around, if you do not see any salespeople, you are the salesperson.

## 12 Man and Machine
- computers are quickly getting better
- globalization helps to substitute for better workers everywhere (?)
- complementary solutions: marking potential fraudulent transfers for review by people
- better computers allows professionals (farmers, lawyers, doctors, ...) to do more work more efficiently
- computers are ideology, and help humans solve harder problems


## 13 Seeing Green
- cleantech bubble
- seven questions every business needs to answer:
> Engineering: Can you create breakthrough technology, instead of incremental improvements?
    -> needs to be better by at least a magnitude (10x) for transparent superiority
> Timing: Is now the right time to start your particular business?
    -> entering a slow moving market might be good, if you have a strategy to take it over
> Monopoly: Are you starting with a big share of a small business?
    -> make sure to dominate meaningful business areas
> People: Do you have the right team?
    -> technologists wear jeans + shirts, pass anyone dressing up for pithes, as the best sales are hidden
> Distribution: Do you have a way to not just create but deliver your product?
    -> make it easy for people to get your product
> Durability: Will your market position be defensible in 10 or 20 years into the future?
    -> have an answer to the question: what will other companies stop to wipe you out?
> Secret: Have you identified a unique opportunity that others do not see?
    -> have a secret, a specific reason for success that other people will not see

> No sector will ever be so important that merely participating in it will be enough to build a great company.

## 14 The Founders Paradox

- Founders have a collection of reinforcing (are they ... because?... or the other way around?) extreme traits
- uniting multiple seemingly complementary traits
- make sure not to 'fall' to exaggerations of them

> The single greatest danger for a founder is to become so certain of his own
> myth that he loses his mind. But an equally insideous danger for every
> business is to lose all sense of myth and mistake disenchantment for wisdom.


# Overall

Wow, what a book. Even thogh most things were pretty much known beforehand, it
is nice seeing them written down like that, there is certainly a few 'basic
general truths' in there. Though it is by far not the only one you would need
to build a business, it gives a good glimpse in how to evaluate businesses, and
maybe stay away from those that will certainly fail. I guess that going by
these recommendations does not ensure success, but it at least makes success
possible.
