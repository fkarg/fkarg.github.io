You know how Schrödingers cat is one of these things people associate with quantum physics? Except for it's not really related.



# Against Schrödingers Cat

(Disclaimer: I am no physicist, and this is merely my understanding of the topic. Please, if you have information that could help me understand/explain this better or if parts are simply wrong, tell me!)

## What is Schrödingers Cat


Schrödingers Cat is (as you might be aware of) a small thought experiment, which is supposed to easily demonstrate how weird quantum physics can get.

The setup is as follows: You have a single atom of some radioactive isotope in a box. Here, you measure with a [geiger counter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geiger_counter) if your isotope has sent out radioactive radiation. There is a mechanism connected to it, so that as soon as this happens, some poison is set free in a (second, bigger) box in which a cat also resides.

Now the poison when set free will kill the cat basically instantly.

Since we can't exactly estimate as to when our [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life) will take place, supposedly, as long as we don't look it up, our cat is in a weird 'superposition' of being both dead and alive at the same time.

![Wikipedia-image of potentially overlapping situations](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Schrodingers_cat.svg)

## More confusion

Honestly, quantum physics is sufficiently confusing the way it is already. The aforementioned quantum superposition is extremely perplexing. Because here an electron is actually in two _seemingly_ mutually exclusive states at the same time (it has proporties of [_both_][1] of these states _at the same time_)! This also works for protons, neutrons, even atoms and [whole molecules](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms1263)! Sure, if you _look at it_ (open the box), you'll ever only get to see it in _one_ of these two states, and you couldn't say beforehand which of these two it'd be in.

See the similiarity? The simplicity? Why some people might like it to introduce other people to the weirdness of quantum physics? Everyone likes cats after all ... But can you also see what _exactly_ the assumptions are this is based upon?

## The least-cat-part (actual physics)

As you must have noticed, the difference is that in the case of our Cat, it is _not_ an actual superposition. We could _not_ design experiments where the cat needs to have the properties of being dead and alive at the same time, _and also_ have our cat-box pass these successfully. Because, after all, these are _definite_ (at least the death one) and utterly _exclusive_ states. The cat itself is at every point only _either_ still alive, _or_ already dead. There is no 'mostly dead' and 'perfectly fine' in the next second. Only alive (which might be followed by) or dead, from which we cannot go back from.

However, for the quantum superposition, even though at every _measured_ point only one of these two states are actually the case, at every point in time before, counterintuitively, _both_ are the case. And don't get me startet on [how][3] [this][4] [can][1] [be][2] [measured][5]. Yes, _both_ states are _simultaniously_ active. Which is to say: If we don't _look_, is has _both_ properties, but as soon as we actually force it to decide (? -> not sure if 'decide' is the correct choice of words here) a state, it will behave _only_ in that way directly afterwards. As if the other never existed. Confused?

## Why Schrödingers Cat is only adding to confusion

So why does it _seem_ to be so similiar? The answer is simple, while in the case of physics, particles can _actually be_ in more than one state at the same time, and we _don't know_ in which of the two states the cat is. Yes, there's some similiarities beyond that (they won't change their state directly afer observing them), for the cat it is only _missing information_ as to which state it is in, whereas in the quantom realm, things can _actually be_ in two _seemingly mutually exclusive_ states.
